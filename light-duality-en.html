The Duality of Light: Between Matter Particle and Probability Wave

Since humanity first looked up at the sky, the eternal question has been: What is light made of? Is it waves rippling through the ether like water? Or is it material particles (tiny spheres) traveling at incredible speeds?

As an engineer, I deal with systems using (0) and (1) logic‚Äîeither current flows or it doesn't. However, in quantum physics, specifically in the "Double-Slit Experiment", this traditional logic collapses.

When we observe light, it behaves as definite material particles. But when we turn our backs and stop observing, it behaves as a wave of probabilities passing through every slit at once! Here lies the philosophical and engineering dilemma: Does the "Observer" create reality?

In the world of programming and engineering, we design systems to be deterministic. But the universe, at its core, seems probabilistic. This contradiction inspires me. The study of light is not just physics; it is a lesson in flexibility‚Äîhow one entity can hold two different natures depending on the context.

Perhaps as engineers, we should learn from light: to be precise like particles in execution, and flexible like waves in thinking.
